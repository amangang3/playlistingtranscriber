Cartelera Teatral Madrileña · Automated Transcription

Turning 270-year-old theater ledgers into a spreadsheet

This tool reads scanned pages from an 18th-century Spanish theater record book and extracts every performance into neat rows, so historians can sort, count, and compare without typing a thing.

The problem

Between roughly 1748 and 1800, Madrid's two main public theaters — Teatro de la Cruz and Teatro del Príncipe — kept meticulous records of every play, every night: the title, the author, the company director, the day of the week, and how much money came in at the door. Those records were later compiled into a printed reference book called the Cartelera Teatral Madrileña.

Today those pages live in PDFs. Each page is a dense, two-column table of plays and dates, printed in tiny type, with section headers, footnotes, and running lists of ticket receipts. A human researcher would need weeks to type one volume into a spreadsheet by hand. This tool does it in an afternoon.

What you put in: a scanned PDF of the Cartelera.
What you get out: an Excel file with one row per performance — 4,000 to 5,000 rows per PDF — ready to filter, sort, or feed into further analysis.

1

Turn each page into a high-resolution picture

The computer opens the PDF and takes a detailed snapshot of every page at 220 DPI — roughly the same resolution as a good magazine scan. This matters because the print is tiny, and a blurry picture would mean the AI can't distinguish 1449 from 1449 (one could be a 4, one could be an 8) or Hidalgo from Holgado.

4

Ask the AI to read the page (twice)

This is the heart of the tool, and it's actually two AI calls per page rather than one.

First pass — the header. The tool crops the top quarter of the image and sends just that slice to the AI with a single question: "Does this page start a new section? If yes, what theater, what season year, and who is the company director?" The zoomed-in crop means the AI sees the (small) director name at full resolution instead of losing it in a forest of play titles. This turned out to be the single most important trick in the whole pipeline — without it, the AI routinely misread director names because they occupy maybe 2% of the page and compete for attention with hundreds of dense receipt numbers.

Second pass — the records. The full page image goes to the AI with the header answer already filled in: "You're looking at Teatro de la Cruz, 1757-1758, director Josef de Parra. Extract every performance row." The AI returns structured data — not a paragraph of text, but a filled-out form for every single performance. A page with one play that ran ten nights becomes ten rows, each with its own date and receipt total.

5

Remember context, resolve shorthand, and save to Excel

Sections in the book span many pages. A director's name might appear once at the top of a section and never again for the next twenty pages. The tool remembers this context: once it sees "Teatro de la Cruz, Josef de Parra", it fills that in for every following performance until a new section header shows up.

It also expands shorthand (see the language of the ledger, below), normalizes theater names to a consistent spelling, and fixes small OCR artifacts. Then it writes everything to an Excel spreadsheet — one row per performance, 17 tidy columns, ready to open.

The language of the ledger

This is the section most people skip, and it's the section that turns a merely-working transcriber into a trustworthy one. The Cartelera uses conventions that a modern reader — and a modern AI — will misread unless you teach the pipeline what they mean.

La misma / El mismo
Literally "the same (feminine) / the same (masculine)". The most treacherous shorthand in the book. When a play ran into a new calendar month, instead of rewriting the full title, the typesetter just wrote La misma. If you take that literally, you get a spreadsheet where a dozen different plays are all called "La misma" — useless for any kind of analysis. The pipeline recognizes this convention and replaces La misma with the actual title from the previous row, but only within the same theater and same section so it never leaks a Cruz play into a Príncipe row.
Multi-date runs (07-12, 14-18)
A single row might list dates like 07-12 to mean the play ran on days 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, and 12 of that month. The tool has to expand that into six separate rows — one per night — and match each date to its own box-office receipt. The AI is explicitly told that the list of receipts below a title is in one-to-one correspondence with the list of dates above it.
Section headers
A section header is the three-line block at the top of a section: the theater name in caps (TEATRO DE LA CRUZ), the season year in brackets ([1757-1758]), and the company director on the next line (Josef de Parra). It appears on the first page of a section and never again, even if the section runs for 30 pages. Every row on those 30 pages inherits the header's context.
Sainetes, entremeses, loas
Each evening at the theater was a multi-piece program: the main comedia plus one or two short accompanying pieces (a sainete or entremés before the play, sometimes a loa). The ledger lists the main play first and the secondary piece indented below. The spreadsheet stores them as Play 1 and Play 2 of the same evening, sharing a date.
The asterisk (*)
An asterisk before a title (*La fe de Abraán) marks a premiere — the first night of that play. Researchers use it to track what was new on Madrid stages. The tool preserves the asterisk rather than stripping it as punctuation noise.
Spanish months
Enero, Febrero, Marzo, Abril, Mayo, Junio, Julio, Agosto, Septiembre, Octubre, Noviembre, Diciembre. The tool keeps dates in the original Spanish format (14 Abril 1748) to preserve fidelity with the source.
Why this matters: the first version of this tool produced a spreadsheet where the single most common play title in the whole book was "La misma". It was 100% accurate at reading the text on the page, and 100% useless for analysis. Understanding the domain language is the difference between an OCR toy and a research instrument.

Why one AI model isn't enough

"Give it to an AI" sounds simple. In practice we evaluated six different vision models from three providers before landing on a combination that worked. Each one failed in a different informative way.

Claude Sonnet 4.6
Strong baseline — 96% title accuracy in early tests. Rejected on cost (~$15 per full PDF).
Gemini 2.5 Flash
Cheap and capable in theory, but suffered multi-hour service outages with repeated 503 errors specifically for vision+JSON requests on paid tier. Unreliable.
Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite
Responded reliably but title quality collapsed to 30%. Too weak to read dense 18th-century print accurately.
Google Cloud Vision API + Claude Haiku 4.5
A two-stage pipeline (purpose-built OCR → LLM structuring). Vision was accurate at reading text but reads tables column-by-column, scrambling the row alignment. Gave up at 61% title accuracy.
Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview
Newest Google model; still hit 503 capacity errors and got worse title accuracy than Flash. Preview-tier models are not production-ready.
OpenAI GPT-5.2
Reliable, strong on dense print, good at following multi-step instructions. Final choice. With header-crop and domain-aware post-processing, delivers 92% title accuracy and 100% director accuracy.

The lesson isn't "GPT is better than Gemini" — it's that no single model is enough. The jump from 61% to 92% didn't come from switching models. It came from realizing that the model was mostly right, and the pipeline around it was wrong.

Why this is genuinely hard

Dense historic print

Each page packs 80–100 performances into two tight columns of 18th-century typography. One wrong character and a whole row slides.

Section headers vanish

Theater and director appear once at the top of a section, then never again. A page opened in the middle has no way to identify itself without remembering the previous section.

Accented Spanish, archaic spelling

Play titles use archaic Spanish orthography and accented characters. Names like María Hidalgo vs Josef de Parra differ by only a few small letters, yet mis-reading one director ruins every row in a 30-page section.

Two theaters, one date

On the same night, Teatro de la Cruz and Teatro del Príncipe ran completely different plays with different directors. You cannot match extracted rows to ground truth by date alone — you must match on theater + date.

Multi-date expansion

A play that ran ten nights appears as one title and a range of dates. The AI has to expand that into ten separate rows with correctly aligned receipts.

Mid-section PDFs

Volume PDFs rarely start at the beginning of a section. The first pages are a continuation with no visible header. The pipeline accepts initial-theater and initial-director hints so the opening rows get labeled correctly.

How accurate is it?

Measured against a hand-typed reference set of 165 performances, the current pipeline (GPT-5.2 with header crop and shorthand resolution) produces these scores:

100%

94%

Matching is performed on the (theater, date) pair, not date alone. Similarity is the character overlap between extracted text and the hand-typed reference. Receipts are scored on exact numeric match.

The remaining 8% of title errors are almost all on long multi-part titles like *El mágico de Salerno, Pedro Vayalarde (2ª parte) where the AI occasionally drops the parenthetical subtitle. These are recoverable with a second pass and will likely get to 96%+ with light prompt tuning.
